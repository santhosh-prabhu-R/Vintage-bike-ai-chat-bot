The Yamaha RX100 was launched in India in 1985 and remained in production until 1996. It features a 98cc two-stroke, air-cooled engine that produces approximately 11 horsepower. The RX100 is famous for its lightweight design, weighing only 95 kg, which gives it incredible acceleration. It is often called the Pocket Rocket because of its high power-to-weight ratio. The iconic pip-pip exhaust sound of the RX100 is due to its unique expansion chamber design.

The Rajdoot 350, popularly known as the RD350, was a licensed version of the Yamaha RD350B. It was the first high-performance motorcycle in India, featuring a 347cc twin-cylinder two-stroke engine. The RD350 came in two versions, the High Torque HT producing 30.5 bhp and the Low Torque LT producing 27 bhp. It can reach a top speed of 140 km/h, making it the fastest vintage two-stroke bike in India.

Jawa motorcycles originated in Czechoslovakia and were brought to India by Ideal Jawa. The Jawa 250 is known for its twin exhaust pipes and a unique gear shifter that also doubles as a kick-starter. The Yezdi Roadking was a successor to the Jawa, featuring a 250cc engine with 16 bhp and a semi-automatic clutch. Yezdi bikes were famous for being rugged and were used extensively in Indian rallies.

The Suzuki Samurai was a 100cc two-stroke bike known for its reliability and smooth engine. It was produced by TVS-Suzuki and was a direct competitor to the Yamaha RX100. The Samurai was marketed as No Problem because of its low maintenance costs. The Kawasaki Bajaj KB100 was another rival, known for having the longest wheelbase in its class and a very comfortable ride quality.

Two-stroke engines complete a power cycle in just two strokes of the piston, up and down. This makes them more powerful than four-stroke engines of the same size. However, two-stroke engines are less fuel-efficient and produce more smoke because they burn a mix of petrol and 2T oil. Most vintage bikes require 20ml to 40ml of 2T oil per liter of petrol to keep the engine lubricated.

The era of two-stroke motorcycles in India peaked between the 1980s and late 1990s. These machines gained a cult following because they offered high power-to-weight ratios that four-stroke engines of that time could not match. The simplicity of their design allowed for easy repairs and modifications, making them favorites for street racing and dirt biking. Brands like Yamaha, Suzuki, and Kawasaki entered the Indian market through collaborations with Escorts, TVS, and Bajaj, respectively, bringing world-class two-stroke technology to the masses.

Unlike a four-stroke engine that requires four movements of the piston (intake, compression, power, and exhaust) to complete one cycle, a two-stroke engine combines these into just two movements. As the piston moves up, it compresses the fuel-air mixture in the cylinder and simultaneously draws a fresh mixture into the crankcase. When the spark plug ignites the mixture, the piston is pushed down, delivering power and pushing the exhaust gases out while pulling the new mixture into the combustion chamber. This every-downstroke-is-a-power-stroke design is why they feel so aggressive and fast.

Two-stroke motorcycles are famous for their power band, a specific RPM range where the engine suddenly produces a massive surge of power. This is often accompanied by a high-pitched, crisp exhaust note and a distinct smell of burning 2T oil. The expansion chamber in the exhaust system is a critical component, it uses pressure waves to stuff unburnt fuel back into the engine, significantly increasing horsepower.

By the early 2000s, two-stroke engines began to disappear from showrooms due to tightening environmental regulations. Because they burn oil along with fuel, they produce higher levels of hydrocarbons and smoke. Additionally, four-stroke technology advanced to become more powerful and fuel-efficient, leading to the eventual ban on the production of new two-stroke road bikes in India.

A critical part of owning a vintage two-stroke is the 2T oil. Since the engine does not have an oil sump like a car, lubrication is provided by mixing oil directly with petrol or using an oil pump. Common maintenance includes cleaning the carbon deposits from the spark plug and the silencer core, as blocked exhausts can significantly reduce performance.
